The question isn't which is better in the abstract. The question is which one solves your specific problem better.

I've used both tools extensively and reached a clear conclusion: Excel and Notion don't compete — they complement each other. But for managing a freelance business, Notion has a structural advantage that Excel simply can't replicate.

## What Excel does well

Excel is unbeatable for complex numerical calculations, data analysis, charts, and spreadsheets with many interconnected formulas. If you need detailed financial projections, data modeling, or working with large volumes of numbers, Excel is the right tool.

It also has the advantage that everyone knows it. If you need to share a quote with a client, an Excel file is universally readable.

## Where Excel falls short for freelancers

Excel's problems appear when you try to manage relationships between information. For example:

You have one sheet for clients and another for projects. You want to see, from a client's record, all their active projects. In Excel, that requires VLOOKUP or INDEX/MATCH formulas that break when you reorganize data. And it doesn't scale.

Or you want to see your projects in Kanban format to know what's in progress. Excel doesn't have that.

Or you want a calendar showing all your project deadlines. Excel doesn't have that either.

## What Notion does differently

Notion is a relational database disguised as a productivity tool. And that characteristic makes it ideal for managing a freelance business.

You can have a Clients database, a Projects database, and an Invoices database, all **related to each other**. When you look at a client, you automatically see all their projects and amounts. When you look at a project, you see which client it belongs to and what invoices are associated.

Plus, you can switch the view of that same information: as a table, Kanban cards, calendar, or gallery. It's the same information organized in whatever way helps you most in each moment.

## The practical conclusion

For **complex financial projections or modeling large volumes of data**: use Excel.

For **managing clients, projects, invoices, follow-ups, and the day-to-day of your business**: Notion is better.

What about quarterly VAT and income tax? People usually assume that means going back to Excel too, but it doesn't have to: with the right database structure, Notion can calculate exactly what you owe each quarter, invoice by invoice.

Most freelancers who try Notion for their management don't go back to Excel for that purpose. Not because Excel is bad, but because Notion is designed for exactly that use case.
